Case 1 - Crisis informatics

When a crisis occur, infrastructure such as Internet or cellular connections may become unreliable, or crises may happen in areas without a proper ICT infrastructure. In this scenario you should imagine an impromptu onsite command centre collecting, curating and creating an overview of the incoming information, using a mix of laptops, tablets, smartphones, and potentially larger displays showing key information and news. How do people bring (digital) information into this context? How do they share information? How could they exploit heterogeneous devices, screens and surfaces at hand?

Case 2 - Interactive Learning

Students of all ages use personal computers. laptops, tables, and smartphones are or will soon be joined by robots (Sphero, LEGO mindstorms etc.), micro-controller based kits (e.g. Arduino) and other interactive installations in school settings. This poses interesting challenges in how educators and learners utilize their personal devices in concert with whatever is installed and available in their particular learning environment. How do devices and infrastructure support transitions between individual-, group- and plenum activities? How do technology support different activities – from learning robotics through co-construction, over creative ideation processes, to collaborative writing and problem solving?

Case 3 - Social Computing

Social computing focus on how technology might support and push social activities outside traditional work settings. The canonical examples are social media, crowdsourcing and gaming. With the proliferation of embedded systems and personal computing there is a huge potential in exploring co-located social computing -- location based games, serendipitous play, second- and multi-screening as part of social events, interaction with public displays and other (more artsy) interactive installations. This scales from collaborating on a shared playlist for a social gathering, over collecting and sharing images and impressions (data) throughout an event to artists incorporating the thousands of devices in use at a concert. What kind of infrastructures and frameworks are needed to support co-located social computing and allow it to scale?

Case 4 - Urban Computing

We are currently seeing a huge focus on embedded technologies in the public space. Data from sensors and embedded systems (utilities, transport, flow/use) are increasingly coupled with public displays and interactive installations in the urban space. Some of this is influencing public participation in decision making (e.g. participatory budgeting and urban planning), and in other cases the data is made available on various open platforms. Engaging with and exploring the particular data in vicinity and appropriating media facades, public displays, NFC capable street furniture, interactive street signage etc., will be an important part of more citizen driven initiatives in the future.
